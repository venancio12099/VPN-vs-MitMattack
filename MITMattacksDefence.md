# Man in the Middle (MitM) Attacks, Analysis and Defence
Man in the Middle (MitM) attack occurs when an attacker intercepts and manipulate the communication between two parties who believe they are interacting directly with each other. By positioning within the communication, attackers can read sensitive information, manipulate data in transit, or poison one of the endpoints. This compromise the confidentiality, integrity, and authenticity of network communications.
MitM attacks are specially dangerous when targeting organisations. A successful interception can compromise confidential data and user accounts inside networks. Beyond the immediate operational impact, these indicident directly affect the reputation of the organization and can result on loss of customer trust and financial consequences. In many cases, MitM attacks serve as the starting point for more advanced threats such as credential based attacks, ransomware, or business email compromise.

# MITM Types
MitM attacks can be executed through several techniques, all aiming to gain control over the communications. One common method is ARP spoofing, where the attacker manipulates ARP responses to mislead devices into the attacker’s MAC address with the IP address of a legitimate gateway or server. Once this manipulation begins, all traffic is flowing through the attacker, who can inspect or modify packets without detection.
Another frequent technique is the creation of an Evil Twin, a false Wi Fi access point configured to replicate a legitimate network. Victims who connect to this fake network expose their traffic to the attacker, who can capture credentials, inject malicious content, or redirect them to phishing pages. A similar concept applies to DNS spoofing, where the attacker manipulates DNS responses to redirect users to fraudulent websites designed to steal information or deliver malware.
More advanced scenarios involve session hijacking, where attackers capture authentication cookies or tokens used to maintain active sessions. Even though these tokens often expire quickly, the attacker can still gain temporary access to sensitive accounts. Another variant is communication hijacking, where attackers compromise or poison communication channels such as email. In many of these cases, social engineering becomes key, attackers often exploit trust to request credentials, payments, or confidential information.

# Defending against MitM attacks
Mitigating MitM attacks require combination of encryption, authentication, and continuous monitoring. Using a VPN is often effective, particularly on public or untrusted networks. Even if an attacker intercepts traffic through ARP spoofing or rogue access points, the encrypted payload remains unreadable.
Equally important is enforcing modern encryption standards such as TLS 1.3. This protocol removes outdated and vulnerable cryptographic algorithms, implements forward secrecy by default, and reduces the handshake process to a single round trip. These improvements significantly reduce the attack surface and make interception or manipulation of encrypted traffic far more difficult.
Multi Factor Authentication (MFA) add another essential layer of protection. Even if an attacker manages to steal credentials or session tokens, they cannot authenticate without the second factor, which typically relies on a physical device or biometric verification.
Finally, active network monitoring is crucial. Detecting anomalies in ARP tables, DNS responses, or traffic patterns allows early identification of spoofing attempts. Automated detection tools can alert administrators to suspicious behaviour before communication is fully compromised, enabling rapid mitigation.

# Conclusion
Man in the Middle attacks are one of the most versatile and dangerous threats in modern networks because they exploit weaknesses in communication channels rather than vulnerabilities in a single device. By intercepting traffic, manipulating data, or impersonating trusted services, attackers can compromise credentials, sessions, and sensitive information with minimal visibility. Understanding how these attacks operate is essential for building effective defensive strategies.
